2A Building the RWD to RWE Engine

Converting standard EMR exchanges from NPHIES into regulatory-grade Real-World Evidence.

01

Continuous Clinical Telemetry

Every consultation on public portals (Sehhaty) and licensed private apps (Cura, NoDiet) streams clinical coding, medication prescriptions, and diagnostics directly to NPHIES Sehey in real-time.

02

The National Pseudonymization Pipeline

Lean Business Services processes raw data, stripping demographic identifiers (National IDs, names) and substituting them with cryptographic hashes. This transforms raw clinical telemetry into Real-World Data (RWD).

03

SFDA Regulatory Enclave

In alignment with the newly outlined SFDA Framework on RWE, longitudinal data sets are organized into patient cohorts. This supports pharmaceutical post-market surveillance, drug efficacy audits, and health economics modeling.

Strategic Objective

To construct the region's largest disease registry (Cardiology, Obesity, Type II Diabetes) using longitudinal clinical insights, turning the Kingdom into a global health-tech powerhouse.

2B Utilizing RWE in Third-Party Apps

How commercial entities (e.g., Cura, Altibbi, NoDiet) leverage centralized data to interpret histories and speed up diagnoses.

Clinical Decision Support (CDSS)

Telehealth platforms query NPHIES Sehey to pull historical patient trends, allowing instant diagnostic matching against verified regional disease protocols.

Personalized Care Pathways

Apps like NoDiet can cross-reference physical pharmacy dispensations (Adam, Nahdi) with active lab reports to optimize weight-management injection plans dynamically.

Automated Risk Stratification

AI systems run localized patient risk scores prior to virtual consultation, flagging pre-diabetic patients to remote clinical pharmacists for medication therapy management (MTM).

API Marketplace ("Heem")

Lean's Heem API marketplace serves as the gateway, allowing validated apps to query specific health parameters securely and uniformly.

The MoH Dilemma

If data is open to *any* private provider or app, how do we guarantee clinical discretion and patient privacy? Uncontrolled data leakage risks exposing highly sensitive medical histories.

Architectural Safeguards

How Discretion is Guaranteed at the Code Level

Third-party private health applications and private hospital groups do *not* have a flat, search-ready interface to browse patient files. Instead, Lean and the MoH enforce an incredibly strict multi-gate validation process.

Federated Query Tokenization (FQT)

Instead of transferring complete clinical folders, third-party apps submit structured queries via Heem. The system utilizes ephemeral, single-use, cryptographically signed JSON Web Tokens (JWT). These expire exactly 15 minutes post-encounter, shutting down long-term credential caching.

Role-Based Specialty Filtering (RBSF)

Anat continuously checks the physician's registered SCFHS specialty. If a remote clinical pharmacist working on NoDiet requests an oncology folder, NPHIES Sehey intercepts the call, throwing an automated block. Practitioners can only request data clinically relevant to their specialty.

PDPL Legal Hammer & Audit Trail

Under the Saudi Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL), every individual lookup must log the practitioner’s ID, the private application's license number, time, and patient OTP. Lean's security operations center (SOC) monitors these files for suspicious patterns, imposing fines of up to SAR 3 million.

Zero-Knowledge Cohort Enclaves

When telehealth platforms wish to query patient records to train diagnostic models or optimize care programs, the algorithm is executed inside a confidential compute sandbox. This means the model processes the anonymized data without the private app ever exporting or viewing patient records.

Gap Analysis & Strategic Brief

5 Saudi Bottlenecks vs. The Western World (US/EU)

Exposing critical developmental bottlenecks in the Kingdom's system and comparing them to US (HIPAA/TEFCA) and European (GDPR/EHDS) frameworks.

Structural Metric The Saudi Bottleneck Western Comparative Benchmark MOH Actionable Solution
1. Deployment Imbalance NPHIES Taameen vs. Sehey Gap: Taameen (Insurance/Claims) is 90% implemented. Sehey (Clinical EHR interchange) lags significantly behind due to fragmented public hospital systems running siloed HIS instances. United States (TEFCA & Meaningful Use): Forced nationwide standard EHR adoption via federal financial incentives and heavy penalties for "information blocking". Tie health-system financial grants and licensing renewals directly to NPHIES Sehey integration milestones. Establish a "Unified National EHR Schema" mandate.
2. Consent Friction & Fatigue Binary Authorization Blocking: Patients must manually provide OTPs for almost every clinical pull. This creates severe friction for emergency triage and chronic care monitoring, causing patients to reject consent to avoid hassle. European Union (EHDS Framework): Introduces an "Opt-Out" model. Patient clinical records are securely available for cross-border care by default, with strict penalty auditing post-access instead of pre-access blocks. Propose a "Tiered Consent Registry" on Sehhaty. Allow citizens to select categories (e.g., share vitals with all licensed private apps, but restrict mental health to explicit OTP only).
3. API Openness & Licensing Siloed Commercial APIs: Private apps (Cura, Altibbi) operate independent, closed databases. They do not cross-exchange records, resulting in patients having duplicate profiles and fragmented histories across different apps. US & UK (FHIR Standard Mandate): The UK NHS and US ONC mandate open HL7 FHIR APIs. Any third-party app with basic credentials can read and write to the patient's record after explicit user OAuth approval. Mandate that any licensed virtual clinic in the Kingdom must utilize standardized HL7 FHIR endpoints to read/write records to NPHIES, eliminating private silos.
4. Practitioner Gig-Economy Barriers Administrative Onboarding Barriers: Practitioners (like clinical pharmacists on Anat) cannot dynamically offer remote consultancy. They must be manually recruited and formally linked to a platform's corporate license in a time-consuming process. Western Freelance Healthcare: Highly optimized digital marketplaces match licensed practitioners with telehealth gaps via standardized, pre-approved credential frameworks. Accelerate Lean's "Muahal" portal into a "National Freelance Telehealth Registry." Allow verified Anat professionals to obtain blanket remote-practice licenses.
Actionable Road Map

Strategic Policy Proposals for MoH Leadership

To cement Saudi Arabia’s position as a global champion in digital health transformation by 2030, the Ministry of Health must implement three primary operational objectives:

01

Incentivize NPHIES Sehey Integration

Introduce operational penalties for public and private healthcare networks failing to submit core diagnostic logs to NPHIES Sehey by Q4 2027. We must address clinical database isolation to build high-quality clinical evidence pipelines.

02

Deploy Dynamic Tiered Consent on Sehhaty

Transition the Kingdom from a restrictive binary consent structure to a tiered, patient-managed system. Citizens should be able to pre-approve private telemedicine portals to view general metrics (e.g. allergies, prescriptions) while locking down highly sensitive logs.

03

Establish the National Clinical Sandbox

Co-develop a state-regulated computing sandbox with Lean Business Services. This will allow certified Saudi pharmaceutical companies and local AI health startups to leverage NPHIES RWD pipelines to develop next-generation diagnostics without breaching patient confidentiality.
